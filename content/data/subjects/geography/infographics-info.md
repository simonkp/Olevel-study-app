#### Topic 2.2 Multiplier Effect & Economic Leakage
### File: tourism-2-2-multiplier-leakage.png

**The same dollar — spent many times vs. spent abroad.**

This companion diagram uses a flowing diagram to contrast two scenarios side-by-side.

* **Left — High multiplier (local economy):** Tourist spends $100 at a locally owned hotel → hotel buys local food → farm employs local workers → workers spend wages at local shops → money stays in the country. Arrows loop inward; total economic impact > $100.
* **Right — High leakage (foreign-owned economy):** Tourist spends $100 at an international chain hotel → hotel imports foreign food and furniture → profits repatriated to HQ abroad → wages paid to expatriate managers who remit money home. Arrows drain outward; total local benefit < $20.

A leakage gauge at the bottom shows the contrast. Callout: developing countries can experience **40–80% leakage**. Strategies to reduce leakage: support local suppliers, train local staff, promote locally owned guesthouses.

#### Topic 3.2 Greenhouse Effect & Enhanced Greenhouse Effect
### File: climate-3-2-greenhouse-effect.png

**The natural greenhouse effect keeps Earth warm. The enhanced version overheats it.**

This infographic is a cross-section diagram of Earth's atmosphere.

* **Natural greenhouse effect (left panel):** Short-wave solar radiation passes through the atmosphere → absorbed by Earth's surface → re-emitted as long-wave infrared → greenhouse gases (H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) absorb and re-radiate some back to surface → keeps Earth ~15°C (vs −18°C without it). Arrows labelled and colour-coded (yellow in, red out, green absorbed).
* **Enhanced greenhouse effect (right panel):** Same diagram but with a thicker atmospheric layer labelled 'excess GHGs (CO₂ from fossil fuels, CH₄ from livestock/landfill, N₂O from agriculture)'. More long-wave radiation trapped → rising global temperatures. A bar chart shows CO₂ concentration rising from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to >420 ppm (2024).
* **Key greenhouse gases table** at the bottom: CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, HFCs — with source icons and global warming potential (GWP) values relative to CO₂.

#### Topic 4.1 Tectonic Plate Boundaries
### File: tectonics-4-1-plate-boundaries.png

**The Earth's crust is broken into moving plates — where they meet, dramatic things happen.**

This poster presents the three types of tectonic plate boundaries, each in its own illustrated cross-section:

* **Convergent (destructive) boundary:** Oceanic plate subducts under continental plate. Features labelled: subduction zone, trench (e.g., Peru–Chile Trench), magma rising through continental crust, composite/stratovolcano, fold mountains. Earthquake foci at shallow, intermediate and deep depths along the Benioff zone.
* **Divergent (constructive) boundary:** Two plates pull apart at a mid-ocean ridge. Features: rift valley, lava erupting to form new oceanic crust, shield volcanoes, shallow-focus earthquakes. Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge; Iceland hot-spot.
* **Conservative (transform) boundary:** Two plates sliding past each other. Features: fault line (e.g., San Andreas Fault), no volcanism, frequent shallow earthquakes. Building damage vs solid bedrock comparison panel.

A world map at the bottom marks the major plates (Eurasian, Pacific, African, Indo-Australian, North/South American, Antarctic) and boundary types in colour.

#### Topic 4.1 Plate Movement & Convection Currents
### File: tectonics-4-1-plate-movement.png

**Plates move because mantle rock circulates like a slow conveyor belt.**

This diagram focuses on the mechanism of plate motion.

* **Cross-section of Earth's layers** (top): labelled crust (oceanic ~7 km thick, continental ~30–70 km), lithosphere, asthenosphere (partially molten), mantle, outer/inner core. Colour-coded.
* **Convection current cells in the mantle** (centre): large red circular arrows showing hot material rising at mid-ocean ridges → spreading outward → cooling and sinking at subduction zones. Labels: *upwelling*, *divergence*, *downwelling*, *convergence*.
* **Ridge push and slab pull forces** (bottom): annotated arrows on a plate cross-section — *ridge push* (newly formed elevated crust slides outward under gravity) and *slab pull* (heavy cold oceanic crust sinks and drags plate). Relative force magnitudes indicated.
* Speed callout: plates move at **1–10 cm/year** — roughly the rate fingernails grow.

#### Topic 4.2 Earthquakes: Causes & Seismic Waves
### File: tectonics-4-2-earthquakes.png

**Earthquakes release energy stored by tectonic stress — in seconds.**

The infographic has three sections.

* **Focus and epicentre diagram:** Cross-section showing stress building up along a fault → sudden slip at the *focus* (hypocentre) → seismic waves radiating outward → surface shaking strongest at the *epicentre* (directly above). Labels: focus depth categories (shallow <70 km, intermediate 70–300 km, deep >300 km).
* **Seismic wave types (side panel):** Two types illustrated with waveform icons:
  * *P-waves (primary)* — longitudinal compression waves; travel through solids and liquids; fastest; arrive first.
  * *S-waves (secondary)* — transverse shear waves; travel only through solids; slower; arrive second.
  * *Surface waves (L and R)* — slowest; cause most damage; roll-like motion (Rayleigh) and side-to-side (Love).
  Seismogram trace at bottom with P and S arrivals labelled; time lag used to calculate distance to epicentre.
* **Measurement scales (bottom right):**
  * Richter Scale — measures magnitude (logarithmic; each unit = 10× energy).
  * Mercalli Intensity Scale — describes shaking effects felt (I = barely felt → XII = total destruction). Comparison side-by-side.

#### Topic 4.3 Disaster Risk Management
### File: tectonics-4-3-disaster-risk.png

**Disaster risk = Hazard × Vulnerability / Capacity.**

The centrepiece is the **Disaster Risk Equation** displayed prominently: *Risk = Hazard × Vulnerability ÷ Capacity*. Each term is defined and illustrated.

* **Hazard**: the natural event (earthquake, volcanic eruption). Magnitude and frequency spectrum shown.
* **Vulnerability**: the susceptibility of a community to harm — linked to poverty, poor building standards, lack of information, dense informal settlements. A 'poverty trap' callout.
* **Capacity**: resources and abilities to cope — government preparedness, early-warning systems, education, strong buildings, international aid networks.

The right half presents the **3-phase DRM framework**:
1. **Before (Preparedness & Mitigation):** Building codes (base isolators, reinforced concrete), hazard mapping, evacuation drills, tsunami warning buoys (DART system), community education.
2. **During (Response):** Search and rescue, emergency shelters, field hospitals, international aid coordination (UN OCHA).
3. **After (Recovery & Reconstruction):** Rebuilding stronger, psychosocial support, insurance, 'building back better' principle.

A case-study comparison panel at the bottom: **Haiti 2010 earthquake** (high vulnerability, low capacity → catastrophic death toll) vs **Christchurch 2011 earthquake** (similar magnitude, higher capacity → far lower fatalities), illustrating why development level shapes disaster outcomes.

#### Topic 5.2 Singapore: Challenges & Opportunities
### File: singapore-5-2-challenges.png

**Small size, no resources — Singapore's existential constraints and pragmatic solutions.**

The infographic is organised around four key resource/development challenges, each with a challenge + opportunity section:

* **Water scarcity:** Challenge — no major rivers or aquifers, dependent on Johor supply. Opportunity/solution — **4 National Taps**: imported water (Johor), local catchment (17 reservoirs), NEWater (purified reclaimed water), desalination. NEWater loop diagram with icons.
* **Land scarcity:** Challenge — 733 km² for 5.9 million people, competing uses. Opportunity/solution — high-rise HDB living (85% of population), land reclamation (+25% area since 1965), underground spaces (Jurong Rock Caverns), vertical farming, mixed-use zoning.
* **Energy dependency:** Challenge — no fossil fuel reserves, import ~95% of energy (mostly piped natural gas from Indonesia). Opportunity/solution — solar PV deployment (HDB rooftop, floating solar on reservoirs), EV transition, smart grid, regional ASEAN Power Grid plans.
* **Population and manpower:** Challenge — low TFR (~1.1), ageing population, foreign worker dependency. Opportunity/solution — structured immigration, Skills Future lifelong learning, retirement age extension, smart automation.

#### Topic 5.3 Singapore's Water Management
### File: singapore-5-3-water-management.png

**From scarcity to security — Singapore's world-renowned water story.**

This infographic traces Singapore's complete water cycle management strategy.

* **Catchment and collection:** Island-wide catchment concept — rain falling anywhere on the island drains into 17 reservoirs. Barrage diagram (Marina Barrage 2008) converting tidal Marina Bay into fresh water reservoir. Central Catchment Nature Reserve protecting water quality.
* **NEWater process flow:** Wastewater → Sewage treatment plant → Microfiltration → Reverse osmosis → UV disinfection → NEWater (ultra-clean reclaimed water). Currently ~40% of Singapore's needs; target 55% by 2060. Main uses: industrial processes, air-con cooling, top-up of reservoirs.
* **Desalination:** Sea water → Reverse osmosis → Fresh water. Two plants (Tuas 1 & 2, Jurong Island). Energy intensive (~3× conventional treatment) — vulnerability noted.
* **Imported water (Johor):** 1961 and 1962 Water Agreements with Malaysia — expire 2011 and 2061. Diplomatic significance; strategic imperative to achieve water self-sufficiency.
* **Demand management:** Tiered pricing, water-saving devices, public education (Water Conservation Awareness campaigns). Consumption reduced from ~170 litres/person/day to target 130 by 2030.

#### Topic 5.3 Sustainable & Resilient Singapore
### File: singapore-5-3-green-resilience.png

**Turning constraints into design — Singapore as a model of urban sustainability.**

This broader sustainability poster covers Singapore's integrated approaches across multiple domains.

* **Green building & urban heat island:** HDB Green Mark scheme, rooftop gardens, sky terraces, vertical greenery, cool paints. Before/after urban temperature comparison; tree-planting programme (Trees.sg) — 300 trees/km² target.
* **Biodiversity and nature conservation:** Central Catchment Reserve, Sungei Buloh Wetland Reserve, Chek Jawa. 'City in a Garden' vision — green corridors linking parks. Species count icons (500+ birds, 260+ butterflies) despite urbanisation.
* **Waste and circular economy:** Zero Waste Masterplan — 70% recycling target. Semakau Landfill (Singapore's only landfill; estimated capacity to 2035). Reverse vending machines, food waste reduction, producer responsibility for e-waste.
* **Coastal protection:** Sea level rise threat — +1 m by 2100 under moderate scenario. Long Island reclamation plan, raised platform levels for new developments, Tuas mega-port built on reclaimed land to 5 m above sea level.
* **Resilience framework (bottom banner):** Four pillars — *Robust* (designed to withstand stress), *Redundant* (backup systems), *Diverse* (multiple supply sources), *Adaptive* (learn and evolve). Singapore's RESILIENCE brand as urban planning model for other city-states.
